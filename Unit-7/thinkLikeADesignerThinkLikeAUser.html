‚óè Lens 01 ‚Äî Psychology

Why do apps feel the way they do?

Great apps aren't just functional ‚Äî they're psychologically engineered. Understanding these principles will sharpen your evaluation enormously.

‚ö°

Cognitive Load

Users can only hold ~4 items in working memory at once. Every extra button, option, or piece of text is a cost. Good apps ruthlessly minimise what's on-screen at any time.

Spotify vs. SoundCloud ‚Äî Spotify's home screen surfaces one action (Play). SoundCloud's feed throws many options simultaneously. Compare how this affects your first 5 seconds of use.
üéØ

Fitts's Law

Bigger targets = faster taps. The time to reach a target depends on its size and distance. This is why play buttons are large and settings are tucked away ‚Äî it's not random.

YouTube vs. Podcasts ‚Äî YouTube's play button dominates the screen. Compare how each app sizes its primary action vs. secondary controls.
üîÑ

The Habit Loop

Trigger ‚Üí Action ‚Üí Variable Reward ‚Üí Investment. Apps like Instagram and TikTok are built around this loop. Understand it, and you can identify it in any app you evaluate.

Instagram vs. Pinterest ‚Äî Both use infinite scroll, but the reward structures differ. Which pulls you back? Why? That's your evaluation gold.
üèÜ

Progress & Completion

Humans hate leaving things unfinished (the Zeigarnik Effect). Profile completion bars, streak counters, and "almost there" nudges all exploit this. It keeps users engaged.

Duolingo vs. Khan Academy ‚Äî Duolingo gamifies streaks aggressively. Khan Academy is more content-first. Compare how each uses progress psychology.
üé®

Visual Hierarchy

Your eye doesn't scan randomly ‚Äî it follows a designed path. Size, colour, contrast, and whitespace all direct attention. A well-designed app guides you to its most important action without you realising it.

Uber vs. Lyft ‚Äî Both do the same thing. Look at what your eye goes to first on the home screen. What's the single dominant action? How is it signalled?

The "5-Second Rule" for Your Evaluation

Open any app cold. Within 5 seconds, can you identify what it does and what the main action is? If not, that's a design failure worth writing about. The best apps answer this instantly ‚Äî through size, colour, position, and layout. Use this as a quick litmus test for every app you evaluate.

What tech is shaping design right now?

Current platform capabilities directly constrain and enable what designers can build. Understanding these is key to the P2/M1/D1 criteria.

üìç

Location & Sensors

GPS ¬∑ Accelerometer ¬∑ Gyroscope

Modern phones are sensor-packed. Apps like Google Maps, Pok√©mon GO, and fitness trackers wouldn't exist without GPS and motion sensors. This tech enables contextual, location-aware features that desktop apps simply can't offer.

Design impact: Features that adapt to where you are

On-Device AI / ML

Edge computing ¬∑ Neural engines

Apple's Neural Engine and Google's Tensor chip run AI models locally. This powers features like real-time translation, photo editing, voice transcription ‚Äî all without an internet connection. It's changing what "offline" means.

Design impact: Instant, private, offline-capable features

Biometrics & Security

Face ID ¬∑ Fingerprint ¬∑ Passkeys

Authentication has shifted from passwords to face and fingerprint. This dramatically simplifies login UX ‚Äî apps like banking and payments now unlock in one gesture. It's a platform capability that reshaped entire interaction patterns.

Design impact: Frictionless security flows
üì∂

Connectivity & Offline Design

5G ¬∑ Progressive Web Apps ¬∑ Caching

5G makes speed less of a constraint, but good apps still design for offline-first. Google Docs, Spotify's download feature, and messaging apps all cache data locally. Connectivity assumptions reveal a lot about design quality.

Design impact: Apps that work anywhere, seamlessly
‚óè Lens 05 ‚Äî Evaluate

Your evaluation framework

Map your analysis directly to the assignment criteria. Strong evaluations don't just describe ‚Äî they justify and suggest.

01

Purpose & Feature Alignment

Does every feature serve the app's core purpose? Or is there bloat? Ask: "Could this app do its job with half the features?" Justify your answer with specific examples.

P1
02

User Needs vs. Implementation

Identify the target user. Then find where the app serves them well ‚Äî and where it fails. Use the persona-thinking from Section 2. Concrete friction points = strong evidence.

P1M1
03

Technology's Role

What platform features does the app exploit ‚Äî or ignore? Compare two apps in the same category: does one use sensors, AI, or offline caching better? That's your tech analysis.

P2M1
04

Comparative Evaluation

Side-by-side is where D1 lives. Don't just describe ‚Äî evaluate effectiveness. Which app does it better? Why? What would you change? Back it up with the psychology and design principles from this guide.

M1D1
05

Justified Improvements

Don't just say "it could be better." Say what you'd change, why, and who it helps. Reference design principles, user psychology, or current tech to justify your suggestions.
